What is a Discount?

A discount is a reduction in the basic price of a product or service. It is usually offered by sellers to increase sales, clear old inventory, or reward loyal customers. Discounts are typically expressed in two ways:

  • Percentage Discount (%): A specific percentage is deducted from the original price (e.g., "20% Off").
  • Fixed Amount Discount: A specific currency amount is deducted (e.g., "₹500 Off").

How to Calculate Discount Manually?

While our calculator does the heavy lifting, knowing the formula is useful for mental math while shopping.

The Basic Formula

Discount Amount = (Original Price × Discount Rate) / 100
Final Price = Original Price - Discount Amount

Example:
You want to buy a pair of shoes priced at ₹2,000 with a 15% discount.

  1. Calculate the discount amount: (2000 × 15) / 100 = ₹300.
  2. Subtract from original price: 2000 - 300 = ₹1,700.
  3. Final Price: ₹1,700.

Understanding Double Discounts (Stacked Discounts)

This is where many shoppers get confused. You often see signs saying "50% + 20% Off". Does this mean a flat 70% discount? No.

Double discounts are applied sequentially, not additively. Here is how it works:

  1. The first discount is applied to the original price.
  2. The second discount is applied to the new, reduced price (not the original).
Example of Double Discount:
Item Price: ₹1,000
Offer: 50% + 20% Off

Step 1: Apply 50% on ₹1,000.
Discount = ₹500. New Price = ₹500.

Step 2: Apply 20% on the new price of ₹500.
Discount = (500 × 20) / 100 = ₹100.

Final Price: ₹500 - ₹100 = ₹400.
Total Savings: ₹600 (which is effectively a 60% discount, not 70%).

Common Types of Discounts

1. Trade Discount

This is a reduction granted by a manufacturer to a retailer. It is usually a percentage off the list price to allow the retailer to make a profit when selling to the end consumer.

2. Quantity Discount (Bulk)

Offered to customers who purchase in large quantities. For example, "Buy 10, get 5% off." This encourages bulk buying.

3. Cash Discount

A reduction in price offered for immediate payment or payment within a specific time frame. Common in B2B transactions (e.g., "2/10 net 30" means a 2% discount if paid within 10 days).

4. Seasonal Discount

Reductions offered on out-of-season merchandise, such as winter jackets sold in summer or air conditioners sold in winter.

5. BOGO (Buy One Get One)

"Buy One Get One Free" is effectively a 50% discount if both items are the same price. "Buy One Get One 50% Off" is effectively a 25% discount on the total purchase.

The Psychology of Discounts

Retailers use discounts as a powerful psychological trigger. The "Anchor Price" (the original high price) makes the discounted price seem like a steal, triggering a fear of missing out (FOMO). Understanding the math helps you look past the marketing hype and evaluate the true value of the deal.

Tips for Smart Shopping

  • Check the Base Price: Sometimes retailers inflate the original price before applying a discount to make the deal look better than it is.
  • Calculate Unit Price: For bulk items, calculate the price per unit/kg/liter to ensure the bulk discount is actually cheaper than buying smaller packs.
  • Factor in Tax: Remember that sales tax (GST/VAT) is usually applied to the final discounted price, not the original price.
