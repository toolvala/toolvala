Preset Modes Explained

  • Eco Mode (JPEG 0.40, scale 2.5×): Targets the smallest possible output. Ideal for meeting strict 100 KB or 200 KB government portal limits. Text remains legible, but fine image details are reduced.
  • Balanced Mode (JPEG 0.60, scale 3.0×): The recommended setting for most users — a strong compression ratio with clear, readable text. Perfect for government forms, bank statements, and application documents.
  • HD Mode (JPEG 0.80, scale 3.5×): Maintains near-original image quality. Best for professional portfolios, certificates, and scanned artwork where clarity matters.
  • Ultra HD Mode (PNG lossless, scale 4.0×): Uses lossless PNG encoding for zero quality loss — every pixel is preserved. Ideal for archival or print-ready documents.

Real-World Example: 15-Page Portfolio

A typical 15-page professional portfolio with scanned certificates and high-resolution project photos is approximately 12 MB when exported from a scanner at full quality. Most government or university application portals cap uploads at 500 KB or 600 KB.

Using Toolvala's Balanced Mode, the same document is re-rendered at 3× DPI with 0.60 JPEG quality. The result: a 480 KB output — over 96% smaller — where all certificate text remains crisp and legible, and project images are still clearly visible for professional review.

Switching to Eco Mode on the same file produces a 180 KB result, safely under a 200 KB limit, while still maintaining readable text throughout the document.

Benefits of Browser-Side Compression

  • Zero Server Upload: Your file never leaves your device. All processing runs in your browser using JavaScript and the PDF.js + pdf-lib libraries. Sensitive documents like bank statements, Aadhar cards, and passports stay completely private.
  • No Watermarks: Unlike many "free" online services, Toolvala never stamps your documents with branding or advertising.
  • Unlimited Pages: Pages are processed sequentially — one at a time — so even 100+ page documents won't crash your browser tab.
  • Grayscale Option: Enabling grayscale converts color images to black-and-white before compressing, typically reducing size by an additional 20–40%. Ideal for forms and text-heavy documents that don't need color.
  • Cross-Platform: Works on Windows, macOS, Android, and iOS. No installation required.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my compressed PDF look blurry with other tools?
Most tools render PDF pages at a low scale (1× or 1.5×) before compressing, which loses sharpness. Toolvala renders at up to 4× scale first, then compresses the high-resolution render — preserving far more detail in the final output.
How do I get under 200 KB or 100 KB?
Start with Eco Mode. If that's still too large, enable Grayscale Mode as well — the combination typically produces the smallest possible output. You can also try Manual Quality and lower it to 0.20–0.30 for maximum compression.
Is there a page limit?
No. The tool processes one page at a time (sequential rendering), so it stays within browser memory limits regardless of document length. 100+ page documents work fine, they just take more time.
Why did my file size increase after compression?
This can happen when the original PDF is a small, pure-vector text document (no images). Rasterizing a vector PDF adds image data overhead. For these files, Ultra HD mode will produce the closest result to the original. If size increase is a concern, skip compression for pure text PDFs.
Is it safe for government IDs and bank documents?
Yes. Toolvala is a zero-server-upload platform. Your files are processed entirely in browser RAM and are never transmitted to any server. The data is permanently gone when you close or refresh the tab.
What's the difference between Grayscale and Eco modes?
Eco Mode lowers the JPEG compression quality and render scale to produce a smaller file. Grayscale Mode removes color information before encoding. They work independently and can be combined for maximum reduction — Eco + Grayscale gives the smallest possible output.
Can I compress encrypted or password-protected PDFs?
No. Encrypted PDFs cannot be parsed without a password. Remove the password protection first using a dedicated tool, then compress the unlocked version here.
