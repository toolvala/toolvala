Why Farm Area Conversion Is So Confusing

Land area measurement in India is a genuine patchwork. The metric system gives us hectares and square metres, British-era surveying left behind acres, square feet and square yards, and traditional regional units like bigha, katha, guntha, biswa and kanal continue to be used in land records, sale deeds and everyday conversation across different states — often with completely different sizes for the same unit name depending on which state or even district you're in. A bigha in Uttar Pradesh is not the same size as a bigha in West Bengal or Rajasthan, and a katha varies just as much. This farm area calculator handles the well-defined international units precisely, and provides a commonly cited reference conversion for bigha and katha with a clear caveat to verify locally.

Standard Land Area Units Explained

Hectare

The metric unit of area, defined as exactly 10,000 square metres, or a square measuring 100m × 100m. It's the standard unit used in most official agricultural statistics and international comparisons.

Acre

A unit from the British imperial system, equal to approximately 4,046.86 square metres or 43,560 square feet. Despite India's official shift to the metric system, acre remains extremely common in land records, real estate listings, and everyday rural conversation.

Guntha

A unit widely used in Maharashtra, Karnataka and parts of southern India, where 40 gunthas make one acre. One guntha equals roughly 101.17 square metres, or about 1,089 square feet.

Bigha

One of the most regionally inconsistent land units in India. In parts of Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, a "pucca" bigha is often cited at roughly 0.625 acre (about 2,529 square metres), while in West Bengal a bigha is considerably smaller, at around 0.33 acre. Rajasthan, Punjab, Haryana, Gujarat and other states each have their own historically established bigha sizes, sometimes varying even within the same state by district.

Katha

Typically a smaller subdivision of a bigha, commonly cited as roughly 1/20th of a bigha in regions where both units are used, though — like bigha — its exact size varies by state and even by local custom.

How to Calculate Area from Field Dimensions

For a roughly rectangular plot, area calculation is straightforward multiplication:

Area = Length × Width

Keep both measurements in the same unit before multiplying — this calculator lets you choose metres or feet, then automatically converts the resulting area into all the other units side by side. For an irregularly shaped field, the most accurate approach is to mentally (or physically) divide the plot into a few rectangular or triangular sections, calculate each section's area separately using this same method, and then add all the section areas together for a reasonably accurate total.

Why Accurate Land Area Matters in Farming

Field area feeds directly into nearly every other calculation a farmer makes — seed rate, fertilizer dose, irrigation water requirement, expected yield, and even lease or rental agreements are all expressed per unit area. A small error in your assumed land area compounds across every one of these downstream calculations, which is why converting confidently between whatever unit your land records use and the unit your other calculations need is genuinely useful, not just a curiosity.

This calculator is intended for quick reference and farm planning purposes only. Bigha and Katha values in particular vary by region and should be confirmed locally. For legal documents, land registration, sale deeds, or property transactions, always rely on an official land survey and your local land revenue records.

Tips for Measuring Your Own Field

  • Use a measuring wheel or long tape for accuracy. Pacing out distances by foot introduces significant error over larger areas.
  • Measure from fixed boundary markers such as survey stones, fence lines, or established field edges rather than estimated points.
  • Split irregular fields into simple shapes. Rectangles and triangles are easy to measure and calculate; irregular curves are not.
  • Cross-check with land records. If you have a sale deed or land record showing area in bigha or another regional unit, use this calculator to sanity-check it against your own dimension-based measurement.
